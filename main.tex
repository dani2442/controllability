\begin{abstract}
    This work investigates the experiment design problem for identifying dynamical systems, with a specific focus on ensuring that learned models capture the controllability properties of the true system. While standard learning approaches often prioritize minimizing trajectory mismatch, we demonstrate that this objective does not guarantee the preservation of control-theoretic properties. For Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) systems, we analyze the conditions under which collected data is sufficiently rich to recover the underlying controllability matrix. We prove that three classes of inputs—polynomial, exponential, and sinusoidal—are persistently exciting, enabling the exact recovery of the controllability matrix rank from terminal states. Furthermore, we establish that a single trajectory generated by a linear combination of these basis functions suffices to recover the full controllable subspace almost everywhere. These results provide theoretical guarantees for designing experiments that yield models suitable for safe and effective downstream control.
\end{abstract}
